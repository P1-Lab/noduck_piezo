<h3>1. The Law of Transduction</h3>

<p>
Transduction is defined not as the capture of “sound,” but as the forensic measurement of mass in motion.
Conventional piezoelectric systems fail by treating vibration as a surface phenomenon rather than a full-body mechanical displacement of the instrument.
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Deterministic Coupling:</strong> Sensor must be mechanically integrated into the instrument chassis to eliminate stochastic “quack” artifacts.</li>
  <li><strong>48 Hz Anchor:</strong> Low-frequency transients represent the fundamental structural mass of the acoustic system and must remain intact.</li>
</ul>

<h3>3. The Law of Boundary Enforcement</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Adhesive Failure Modes:</strong> Soft adhesives introduce high-pass filtering effects, removing low-frequency mass content and generating high-frequency artifacts.</li>
  <li><strong>Structural Mandate:</strong> The sensor must be rigidly coupled to the instrument chassis via high-modulus bonding.</li>
  <li><strong>Wood-on-Wood Interface:</strong> Mandatory coupling layer ensuring phase coherence between mechanical body and transducer base.</li>
</ul>

<hr>

<h3>4. Forensic Placement Protocol</h3>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Mechanical Impulse Test:</strong> Excite the instrument and identify maximum displacement zones using reference sensing methods.</li>
  <li><strong>Harmonic Audit:</strong> Avoid nodal cancellation zones where destructive interference suppresses the 48 Hz structural anchor.</li>
  <li><strong>Vitrification Check:</strong> Confirm linear response post-installation without electronic compensation or corrective processing.</li>
</ol>
